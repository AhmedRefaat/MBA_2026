# Lec-6: Leading

## Leadership Styles - One Slide Summary

| Style | Core Idea | When to Use | Advantages | Disadvantages |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **1. Autocratic Leadership** | Leaders make decisions without consulting their team members, even if their input would be useful; this leader makes all decisions and controls all aspects of a group or organization without consulting or involving others; this style is directive, task-oriented, and highly centralized. | When dealing with inexperienced or unmotivated team members; when there is an urgent need for quick decisions; in situations where the leader has more expertise; in times of crisis, where quick decisions need to be made to protect the organization and its members. | Quick decision-making; clear direction and expectations; effective in crisis situations. | High levels of absenteeism, stress, dissatisfaction, and staff turnover; limited creativity and innovation, which can lead to missed opportunities for growth and development; lack of trust between the leader and team members. |
| **2. Democratic Leadership** | The leader involves team members in the decision-making process and encourages them to share ideas, opinions, and perspectives, but the responsibility for making the final decision still rests with the leader; this style is characterized by collaboration, open communication, and teamwork. | When team members have expertise; for projects requiring creativity and teamwork, such as marketing campaigns; in stable environments with time for deliberation. | Boosts employee morale because employees contribute to decision-making; encourages creativity, and people are often highly engaged in projects and decisions; team members tend to have high job satisfaction and high productivity. | Slower decision-making; can be ineffective in high-pressure situations. |
| **3. Laissez-Faire Leadership** | The leader delegates decision-making power to team members and provides minimal guidance or supervision; the essence of this style is that the group itself outlines objectives and creates the conditions for pursuing those goals. | For creative or research-driven projects; when leading highly skilled professionals; when the leader is not an expert; when flexibility is important, because team members can adapt to changing circumstances and make decisions quickly. | High level of job satisfaction, innovation, and creativity; subordinates can focus on the areas where they are most talented instead of only doing what they are told. | Can lead to lack of direction or accountability. |
| **4. Team Leadership** | A team leader is responsible for managing and leading a team of employees toward achieving a specific goal or objective; the role of a team leader is critical in ensuring that team members are motivated, engaged, and working toward organizational goals; team leadership is about working with the hearts and minds of all those involved. | For new or inexperienced teams, where members need guidance and help working together effectively; for complex or challenging tasks, where the team needs resources and support to succeed. | Improved team performance by helping members work together effectively and efficiently toward organizational goals; better communication because the team leader acts as a communication bridge between team members and upper management through updates, feedback, and guidance. | Time-intensive; may prioritize relationships over results. |
| **5. Transactional Leadership** | Leaders guide or motivate followers toward established goals by clarifying roles and task requirements; this style focuses on clear goals, structures, and rewards; leaders establish expectations and provide feedback based on performance. | For routine and predictable tasks; in high-stress environments such as emergency services, where order and established procedures matter; for short-term projects, where tasks must be completed on time and within budget; for newly formed teams, where clear roles and responsibilities are needed; in performance-based work environments such as sales, where incentives and rewards motivate employees. | Efficient and results-oriented; clear expectations; good for short-term goals. | Can stifle creativity and innovation; may lead to a focus on individual rewards over collective goals. |
| **6. Transformational Leadership** | Inspires and motivates followers to achieve extraordinary outcomes and develop their own leadership capacity; leaders act as role models, foster a sense of shared purpose, and encourage innovation. | When creativity, innovation, and change are required; when leading highly skilled and motivated individuals who are willing to take risks and work toward a common goal; in industries such as technology, design, and creative fields where innovation is highly valued; when the organization is undergoing significant change, such as mergers or acquisitions, and employees need inspiration during the transition. | High employee engagement; increased motivation; strong team cohesion; promotes creativity. | Can be ineffective in crisis situations requiring quick decisions; potential for burnout if leaders are not mindful of their own well-being. |
| **7. Charismatic Leadership** | An enthusiastic and self-confident leader whose personality and actions influence people to behave in certain ways. | During times of change and uncertainty; when a strong sense of direction and motivation is needed; in organizations that require significant transformation or are facing challenging circumstances. | High employee engagement and motivation; strong team cohesion; increased productivity and performance. | Dependence on the leader, making succession planning difficult; potential for blind faith, where followers overlook flaws or make poor decisions because of strong belief in the leader; difficulty with criticism, as charismatic leaders may not be receptive to feedback or dissenting opinions. |

## Quick Memory Line

- **Autocratic** = leader decides alone
- **Democratic** = team participates, leader decides finally
- **Laissez-Faire** = team decides with minimal supervision
- **Team Leadership** = leader coordinates people toward a shared goal
- **Transactional** = goals, roles, rewards, and performance
- **Transformational** = inspiration, change, and innovation
- **Charismatic** = influence through personality and confidence

## Conflict Types - One Slide Summary

| Conflict Type | What It Is | Example | How to Deal With It |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **3. Task Conflict** | Conflicts over work content and objectives — what needs to be done, priorities, goals, or the "what" of the project. Usually cognitive (idea-based) rather than emotional. Can be healthy when kept professional. | Two team members disagree on whether to focus on feature A or feature B first. | **Encourage healthy debate:** Use structured techniques like "Six Thinking Hats" or Devil's Advocate rounds. **Clarify objectives early:** Run a quick alignment session with the question "What problem are we actually solving?" **Facilitate, don't dictate:** As leader, stay neutral, summarize both sides, then guide the team to data-driven decisions. **Outcome goal:** Convert the conflict into better decisions and innovation. |
| **4. Relationship Conflict** | Conflicts based on interpersonal dynamics — personalities, values, communication styles, or personal dislikes. Highly emotional and often the most damaging if left unaddressed. | Tension between two colleagues who simply don't "click" or have clashing egos. | **Address privately and early:** Use the "I notice… I feel… I need…" script in a 1:1 conversation. **Focus on behaviors, not personalities:** Say "I notice the tone in meetings has become sharp" instead of "You're aggressive." **Mediate with empathy:** Bring both parties together only after individual check-ins; use active listening and mutual respect agreements. **Build psychological safety:** Run team-building activities or anonymous feedback rounds to strengthen relationships proactively. **When severe:** Consider temporary role separation or, in extreme cases, reassignment. |
| **5. Process Conflict** | Conflicts over how the work is executed — methods, procedures, responsibilities, timelines, or resource allocation. Often appears as logistical friction but can quickly turn personal if roles feel unclear. | "Should we use Agile or Waterfall?" or "Who owns the final sign-off?" | **Map the process visually:** Use a RACI chart (Responsible, Accountable, Consulted, Informed) or simple workflow diagram in a 30-minute meeting. **Agree on rules of engagement:** Create a short "Team Process Charter" that everyone signs off on. **Revisit regularly:** Schedule a 15-minute "Process Check" at the start of each sprint or quarter. **Outcome goal:** Remove ambiguity so energy returns to the actual work. |

## Conflict Types Quick Memory

- **Task Conflict** = WHAT (what to build or prioritize)
- **Relationship Conflict** = WHO (who is clashing)
- **Process Conflict** = HOW (how to do the work)
