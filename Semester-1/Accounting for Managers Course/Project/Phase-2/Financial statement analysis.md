# Analysis of Financial Statements

Project – Phase II

## Purpose of Analysis

Common goal of financial statement analysis for both external and internal users is to evaluate company performance and financial condition and to assist in evaluating:

- Past and current performance
- Current financial position
- Future performance and risk

**Internal Users:** Managers, Officers, Internal Auditors

**External Users:** Shareholders, Lenders, Suppliers

## Building Blocks of Analysis

1. **Liquidity and efficiency**
2. **Solvency**
3. **Profitability**
4. **Market prospects**

## Standards for Comparison

When we interpret our analysis, it is essential to compare the results we obtained to other standards or benchmarks:

- **Intracompany** — compare within the same company over time
- **Competitor** — compare against direct competitors
- **Industry** — compare against industry averages
- **Guidelines** — compare against established rules of thumb

## Financial Ratio Analysis

Financial ratio analysis is the use of relationships among financial statement accounts to gauge the financial condition and performance of a company.

---

## First: Liquidity Analysis

Liquidity is the ability to satisfy the company's short-term obligations using assets that can be most readily converted into cash.

**Working Capital** = Current Assets - Current Liabilities

### Current Ratio

$$\text{Current Ratio} = \frac{\text{Current Assets}}{\text{Current Liabilities}}$$

- Measures the ability of the company to pay current debts as they become due.
- A ratio greater than 1 implies that current assets are sufficient to cover current liabilities.
- If less than 1, analysts will want to understand how the company intends to meet its short-term obligations.
- Home Depot example: had $1.17 in current assets for each $1.00 in current liabilities.

### Quick Ratio

$$\text{Quick Ratio} = \frac{\text{Cash + Net Accounts Receivable + Marketable Securities}}{\text{Current Liabilities}}$$

- A more stringent test of short-term liquidity than the current ratio.
- Quick assets = cash and near-cash assets (net accounts receivable and marketable securities).
- **Excludes:** Inventory and Prepaid expenses.
- Home Depot example: 34 cents in cash and near-cash assets for every $1.00 in current liabilities.

### Cash Ratio

$$\text{Cash Ratio} = \frac{\text{Cash + Cash Equivalents}}{\text{Current Liabilities}}$$

- Measures the adequacy of available cash.
- Home Depot example: 22 cents of cash for each $1.00 of current liabilities.

## Second: Operating Cycle (Activity) Ratios

The operating cycle is the time it takes for a company to pay cash to its suppliers, sell goods to its customers, and collect cash from its customers.

The operating cycle for most companies involves three distinct phases:

1. The acquisition of inventory
2. The sale of the inventory
3. The collection of cash from the customer

### Accounts Receivable Turnover Ratio

$$\text{Receivable Turnover} = \frac{\text{Net Credit Sales (or Net Sales)}}{\text{Average Accounts Receivable}}$$

- Measures how quickly a company collects its accounts receivable.
- A high ratio suggests the company collects receivables many times during a year.
- Home Depot example: collects receivables over 50 times a year.

### Average Days to Collect Receivables

$$\text{Days to Collect} = \frac{365}{\text{Receivable Turnover Ratio}}$$

- Home Depot example: takes an average of just over 7 days to collect its accounts receivable.

### Inventory Turnover Ratio

$$\text{Inventory Turnover} = \frac{\text{Cost of Goods Sold}}{\text{Average Inventory}}$$

- Measures how quickly the company sells its inventory.
- An increase in this ratio is usually favorable.
- If the ratio is too high, it may indicate lost sales because desired items were not in stock.
- Home Depot example: inventory was acquired and sold just over 5 times during the year.

### Average Days to Sell Inventory

$$\text{Days to Sell} = \frac{365}{\text{Inventory Turnover Ratio}}$$

- Home Depot example: on average, takes just over 69 days to sell its inventory.

### Accounts Payable Turnover

$$\text{AP Turnover} = \frac{\text{Purchases (or COGS)}}{\text{Average Accounts Payable}}$$

- Measures how quickly management is paying its suppliers.
- A high ratio suggests the company is paying suppliers in a timely manner.
- A decreasing ratio could signal the company is taking longer to pay off suppliers (financial distress or renegotiated payment terms).

### Average Days to Pay Payables

$$\text{Days to Pay} = \frac{365}{\text{Accounts Payable Turnover Ratio}}$$

### Operating Cycle and Cash Cycle

$$\text{Operating Cycle} = \text{Average Days to Sell Inventory} + \text{Average Days to Collect Receivables}$$

$$\text{Cash Conversion Cycle} = \text{Operating Cycle} - \text{Average Days to Pay Payables}$$

**Example:** If a company has average age of inventory = 120 days, average age of AP = 100 days, average age of AR = 40 days:

- Operating Cycle = 120 + 40 = **160 days**
- Cash Cycle = 160 - 100 = **60 days**

## Third: Solvency Analysis

Solvency refers to the long-run financial viability and the ability to cover long-term obligations.

- **Business risk** is determined from the company's line of business.
- **Financial risk** is the risk from a company's choice of financing (debt vs equity).

Two types of solvency ratios:

1. **Capital Structure** — evaluating elements in the capital structure and their attributes.
2. **Earning power** — recurring ability to generate cash from operations.
3. **Loan covenants** — protection against insolvency and financial distress.

### Debt-to-Equity Ratio

$$\text{Debt-to-Equity} = \frac{\text{Total Liabilities}}{\text{Total Stockholders' Equity}}$$

- Measures the amount of liabilities for each $1 invested by the owners.
- Home Depot example: for each $1.00 of stockholders' equity, had $29.63 of liabilities.

### Times Interest Earned Ratio

$$\text{Times Interest Earned} = \frac{\text{Income Before Interest and Taxes}}{\text{Interest Expense}}$$

- Compares income available to pay interest to the interest obligation for the same period.
- Indicates a margin of protection for creditors.
- A high ratio indicates a secure position for creditors.
- Home Depot example: generated $13.96 in income for each $1.00 of interest expense.

## Fourth: Profitability Ratios

### Gross Profit Percentage

$$\text{Gross Profit \%} = \frac{\text{Gross Profit}}{\text{Net Sales}} \times 100$$

- Reflects gross profit as a percent of sales.
- Gross Profit = Net Sales - Cost of Sales.
- Home Depot example: approximately 34 cents of each dollar of sales remaining to cover other expenses.

### Net Profit Margin

$$\text{Net Profit Margin} = \frac{\text{Net Income}}{\text{Net Sales}} \times 100$$

- Reflects net income as a percentage of sales.
- Home Depot example: each dollar of sales generated over 8 cents of profit.

### Return on Assets (ROA)

$$\text{ROA} = \frac{\text{Net Income}}{\text{Average Total Assets}}$$

- Compares income to total assets used to generate it.
- Home Depot example: for every $1.00 of assets, earned just under 20 cents.

### Return on Equity (ROE)

$$\text{ROE} = \frac{\text{Net Income}}{\text{Average Stockholders' Equity}}$$

- Relates income earned to the investment made by the owners.
- Home Depot example: for every $1.00 equity investors contributed, earned $2.98.

### The DuPont Model

The DuPont model is used by analysts to better assess how a company is implementing its business strategy. It breaks ROE into three components:

$$\text{ROE} = \text{Net Profit Margin} \times \text{Total Asset Turnover} \times \text{Financial Leverage Multiplier}$$

$$\text{ROE} = \frac{\text{Net Income}}{\text{Sales}} \times \frac{\text{Sales}}{\text{Total Assets}} \times \frac{\text{Total Assets}}{\text{Equity}}$$

## Fifth: Investor and Market Analysis

### Earnings per Share (EPS)

$$\text{EPS} = \frac{\text{Net Income}}{\text{Average Number of Common Shares Outstanding}}$$

- Probably the single most widely reported financial ratio.
- The only ratio required by GAAP.
- Average number of shares outstanding is reported on the bottom of the income statement.

### Price/Earnings (P/E) Ratio

$$\text{P/E Ratio} = \frac{\text{Market Price per Share}}{\text{Earnings per Share}}$$

- Reflects the stock market's assessment of a company's future performance.
- A high ratio indicates that earnings are expected to grow rapidly.
- Home Depot example: stock was selling at a price that was just over 28 times its EPS.

### Dividend Yield Ratio

$$\text{Dividend Yield} = \frac{\text{Dividends per Share}}{\text{Market Price per Share}}$$

- Reflects the return on investment solely due to dividends.
- Investors accept low dividend yields if they expect stock price to increase.
- Stocks with high dividend yields often appeal to retired investors who need current income.

### Quality of Income

$$\text{Quality of Income} = \frac{\text{Cash Flows from Operating Activities}}{\text{Net Income}}$$

- A ratio higher than 1 indicates high-quality income because each dollar of income is supported by one or more dollars of cash flows.
